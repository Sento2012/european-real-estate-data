The biggest surprise in this June 2026 snapshot is how far apart “affordable” can still be within the same apartment category. Newcastle upon Tyne is the cheapest city in the sample at €230,784 for a 3-room apartment, while Leeds climbs all the way to €419,456 — a gap of nearly €190,000 for broadly similar stock.

That low-end cluster is pretty compelling for movers and family buyers. Plymouth comes next at €240,892, followed by Glasgow at €257,737. Even Sheffield and Cardiff stay below €300,000, at €294,799 and €298,167 respectively, which keeps them in reach for buyers trying to stay under a tighter budget.

The rental side tells a different story. Newcastle pairs that low asking price with a strong €1,874 monthly rent and a 9.74% gross yield, while Glasgow posts €2,075 and 9.66%. By contrast, Plymouth’s much lower €707 rent translates to just 3.52% yield, showing that cheap entry prices don’t always mean strong investment returns.

For anyone comparing relocation options or investment markets, the takeaway is simple: the cheapest cities aren’t just cheaper — they can also deliver very different income profiles. That makes local context matter just as much as headline price.
