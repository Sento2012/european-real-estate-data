The most surprising takeaway is that shrinking Spanish cities don’t all look “cheap” at all. Cádiz, for example, has lost 4.75% of its population over five years, yet apartments still command a median asking price of €261,229 — with a relatively low gross yield of 3.54%. That’s a very different picture from Linares, where a 3.00% population decline coincides with a far lower median price of €80,566 and a much stronger 7.98% yield.

That split is the real story here: demographic decline doesn’t automatically translate into distressed pricing. In some places, like Cádiz and Vigo, prices remain elevated even as population trends soften. In others, weaker demand has pushed entry costs down enough to make the rental math more attractive for investors.

For anyone tracking Spain’s regional housing market, the lesson is to look beyond population alone. Ferrol, for instance, sits in the middle ground with a median price of €129,393 and a 5.88% yield, showing how local market structure can matter as much as demographic momentum.
