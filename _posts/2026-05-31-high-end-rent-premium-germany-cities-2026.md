The most surprising takeaway is that the biggest gap between “typical” and high-end apartment rents isn’t limited to Germany’s priciest markets. Rostock, a comparatively mid-priced city, actually shows the widest premium at the top end: the median asking rent is €521/month, while the 75th percentile jumps to €899/month.

That kind of spread suggests a sharply split rental market, where premium listings behave almost like a separate submarket. Wiesbaden and Frankfurt am Main are close behind. In Wiesbaden, the median sits at €1,000/month and the upper quartile reaches €1,712/month, while Frankfurt rises from €1,068/month at the median to €1,826/month at the 75th percentile.

The pattern matters for both renters and investors. A city can look moderate on headline rents but still have a very expensive top tier. Rostock’s upper-band premium is 72.6%, Wiesbaden’s is 71.2%, and Frankfurt’s is 71.0% — strong evidence that premium stock is pulling away from the mainstream in these markets.
