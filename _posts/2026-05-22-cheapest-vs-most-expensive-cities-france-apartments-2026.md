The most striking takeaway from France’s 2026 apartment snapshot is just how far the market splits between value and prestige. Saint-Quentin sits at only €60,057, while Neuilly-sur-Seine reaches €944,823 — a gap that shows these cities aren’t just different price points, but different investment worlds.

The cheapest trio all comes in under €90,000: Saint-Quentin (€60,057), Saint-Étienne (€86,425), and Belfort (€89,355). At the other end, the expensive group starts at €549,316, with Paris and Levallois-Perret both at that level before Neuilly-sur-Seine jumps to nearly €1 million. That’s a huge spread, especially when you consider that Paris has 2,243,833 residents, while Levallois-Perret and Neuilly-sur-Seine are compact, high-demand suburbs with around 64,253 and 61,754 residents.

What does that mean for buyers? In short: lower entry prices tend to come with stronger gross yields, while the priciest inner-metro markets deliver prestige more than income. For investors, the cheap end of the market looks far more efficient on returns; for owner-occupiers, the top end is all about location and status.
