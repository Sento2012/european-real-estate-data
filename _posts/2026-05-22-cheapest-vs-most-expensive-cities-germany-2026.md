The most surprising finding? Germany’s cheapest apartment cities aren’t just cheaper — they’re also delivering noticeably stronger rental yields than the pricier metros. In the 16 May 2026 snapshot, Chemnitz leads the low-cost group with a median asking sale price of just €67,443, while München sits at the top end at €644,225. That’s a huge gap in entry cost, and it changes the investment math fast.

The budget end of the market is dominated by Chemnitz, Duisburg, and Gelsenkirchen. Chemnitz pairs that €67,443 price tag with a median rent of €346/month and a gross yield of 6.16%. Duisburg isn’t far behind at €99,791 and 6.14%, showing how lower purchase prices can translate into better income efficiency.

At the premium end, Freiburg im Breisgau, Frankfurt am Main, and München stand out for all the usual reasons: strong demand, high prices, and tighter returns. München’s €644,225 median asking price is the clearest sign of how expensive core German cities have become, even when rents remain solid. For buyers and relocators, the takeaway is simple: city choice matters more than many market tweaks.
