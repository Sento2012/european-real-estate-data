The most surprising finding? In Madrid, apartments didn’t just cost far less than houses — they also delivered a much better rental return. On the 6 Jun 2026 snapshot, flats posted a gross yield of 4.13%, compared with just 2.75% for houses. That makes apartments look like the more efficient choice for investors focused on income per euro invested.

The numbers behind that gap are pretty striking. The median asking price for an apartment was €524,901, with median rent at €1,805/month. Houses were in a completely different bracket: €1,701,659 median asking price and €3,896/month median rent. So while houses brought in more rent in absolute terms, the extra income didn’t come close to offsetting the much higher purchase price.

This also says a lot about Madrid’s housing stock. The city’s listing mix is heavily tilted toward flats, which is typical for a dense capital where apartments dominate the mainstream market and houses tend to be rarer, larger, and more premium. For buyers weighing format over neighbourhood, the takeaway is simple: apartments offered the stronger yield story, while houses looked more like a luxury play than a return-focused one.
