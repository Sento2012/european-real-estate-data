The most surprising thing about Italy’s housing market is how orderly the last decade looked: no dramatic crash, just a long, shallow dip followed by a steady climb to new highs. By Q4 2025, the national house price index reached 118.10, marking a clear break above both the late-2010s low and the earlier mid-decade levels.

That downturn was real, though. Italy’s index slipped from 100.70 in Q3 2016 to 97.70 in Q1 2019, with annual growth negative for much of that stretch. Then the market turned: the recovery began in 2020 and gathered pace through 2024 and 2025, showing that the biggest story here is the shift in direction, not quarter-to-quarter volatility.

What stands out now is how far the rebound has carried prices. After spending years below 100, the index is now comfortably above 118, reflecting a market that reset rather than broke. For buyers and observers alike, Italy looks less like a boom-bust case and more like a slow-motion recovery that finally got its footing.
