The biggest surprise in France’s 2026 housing data isn’t that Paris is expensive — it’s just how extreme the gap has become. Paris sits at a median **€11,000/m²**, while cities like **Saint-Étienne** and **Mulhouse** are below **€1,000/m²**. That means the same budget can buy a studio in the capital or a much larger family home in a secondary city.

The top end is still dominated by the Paris region. **Boulogne-Billancourt** comes in at **€9,125/m²**, followed by **Versailles** at **€7,270/m²** and **Nanterre** at **€5,440/m²**. Paris also stands out for how little space buyers get: the average property size is just **18 m²**, compared with **27 m²** in Versailles and **39 m²** in Nanterre.

On the other side of the market, affordability opens up very different opportunities. In the cheapest cities, buyers are looking at prices that can be a fraction of Paris levels, making them attractive for first-time buyers and value-focused investors. The takeaway is simple: in France, location still drives everything — but the spread in 2026 is wide enough to reshape what “affordable” really means.
