The most surprising part of this ranking isn’t just how cheap the apartments are — it’s how different the “cheap” markets can be. In the 16 May 2026 snapshot, Saint-Quentin tops the affordability list at a median asking price of just €62,987, but that low price reflects a very different market than larger places like Saint-Étienne or Limoges.

Saint-Étienne comes in at €89,355, while Belfort sits at €92,285. By the time you reach €98,144, you’re looking at a tie between Marseille 3e, Troyes and Limoges. Those similar price points hide very different local dynamics: Saint-Étienne has 1,018 listings and a population of 171,260, while Saint-Quentin has 134 listings for 56,217 residents.

That’s the key takeaway for budget-first buyers: a sub-€100,000 apartment doesn’t always mean the same opportunity. In some cities, it signals broad market access with plenty of inventory; in others, it may simply reflect a smaller, thinner market. Looking at listings alongside prices helps separate true value from limited supply.
