The most surprising takeaway from the data is that Madrid’s high prices don’t signal a fragile market—they may actually reflect strength. At first glance, the Spanish capital looks expensive, but the numbers suggest a city with deep demand, strong liquidity, and plenty of buyer interest.

Madrid stands out on scale alone: it has **34,158 listings**, more than double the Italian comparison market’s **14,633**. That’s a gap of **19,525 listings**, which points to a much more active and competitive market. Pricing tells the same story. Madrid’s **average asking price is €904,549**, versus **€431,864** in Italy, while the **median** is **€490,000** compared with **€284,000**.

What makes this especially interesting is the combination of inventory and pricing power. Markets with lots of listings can sometimes be soft—but Madrid is holding premium values at the same time. That suggests broad demand, not just a few outlier luxury homes, and it makes the city look less like an overheated market and more like one with resilient fundamentals.

For investors, the lesson is clear: lower-priced markets aren’t automatically better bargains, and expensive ones aren’t automatically overvalued. Sometimes, pricing is simply a reflection of depth, liquidity, and stronger buyer competition.
