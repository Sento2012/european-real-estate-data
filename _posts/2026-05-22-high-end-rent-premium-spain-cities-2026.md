The most surprising finding is that the biggest gaps between “normal” and high-end rents aren’t in Spain’s biggest cities — they’re in smaller, more segmented markets. Águilas leads the pack, where the 75th-percentile asking rent hits €2,023/month, more than double its €1,005 median. That’s a 101.3% premium at the top end, which is a huge signal that premium listings are scarce and very different from the rest of the market.

Gandia and Cullera show the same pattern, just a bit less extreme. In Gandia, the median rent is €907/month, but the upper quartile jumps to €1,718/month, an 89.4% premium. Cullera follows with a €1,005 median versus €1,757 at the 75th percentile, a 74.8% gap. In all three cities, the spread suggests a rental market split between mainstream stock and a small set of much pricier homes.

Barcelona is still important, though, because it combines a large listing base with a notably wide premium at the top end. The takeaway for renters and investors: headline averages can hide a lot. What matters here is how sharply a city’s rental stock is divided — and in these markets, the divide is unusually pronounced.
