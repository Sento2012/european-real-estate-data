Berlin’s biggest surprise is that the weakest rental returns weren’t in the largest apartments — they were in the middle. In April 2026, Berlin’s 4-room homes posted the city’s lowest gross yield at 2.59%, edging out 3-room units at 2.64%. That’s a reminder that “bigger” doesn’t always mean “better” for income investors.

At the top of the chart, 1-room apartments led comfortably with a 3.55% gross yield. They paired a median asking sale price of €199,278 with median asking rent of €590/month, which helps explain why compact units keep outperforming on a percentage basis. Two-room apartments followed at 2.99%, with a median sale price of €318,907 and rent of €795/month.

What makes Berlin especially interesting is the rebound at the very large end. 5+ room apartments recovered to a 3.00% yield, suggesting that ultra-large stock can hold up better than the mid-sized family segment. In other words, the yield curve is not a straight line — it dips in the middle and then firms up again.
