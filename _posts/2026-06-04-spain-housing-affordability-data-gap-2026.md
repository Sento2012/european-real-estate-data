Spain’s biggest affordability surprise isn’t just how expensive the market is — it’s how quickly the price data has moved past the income data. The latest asking-price snapshot is from 30 May 2026, while the official income reference is still the 2023 INE Atlas, so buyers are effectively comparing today’s listings with a picture that’s already two to three years old.

And those listings are eye-opening. Calvià leads the apartment market at €725,097, followed by Ibiza at €705,566 and Sitges at €646,972. Even major urban markets are far from cheap: Madrid sits at €534,667 and Palma de Mallorca at €524,901.

On the income side, the gap is just as important. Mean household income in this dataset ranges from €30,717 in Calpe / Calp to €52,067 in San Sebastián - Donostia, with Ibiza at €50,627 and Madrid at €49,916. That doesn’t make the numbers useless — it just means affordability needs to be read as a moving target, not a fixed ratio.

The takeaway: Spain’s housing conversation is being shaped by fast-moving asking prices and slower-moving official income stats. That mismatch matters, especially in the country’s priciest coastal and city markets.
