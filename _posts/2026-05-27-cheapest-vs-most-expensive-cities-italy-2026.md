The most surprising takeaway from Italy’s 2026 apartment snapshot is just how far prices split between cities: you can still find median asking prices under €90,000 in places like Taranto, Messina and Livorno, while Bolzano is nearly half a million euros at €493,651. That’s not a small difference — it’s a completely different market reality depending on where you buy.

At the bottom end, Taranto leads the affordability pack at €83,495, with Messina and Livorno both at €89,355. On the high end, Milano comes in at €373,535, Firenze at €414,550, and Bolzano tops the list at €493,651. In other words, the gap between the cheapest and priciest cities is more than €400,000.

The rental side tells a similar story, but with better yield potential in cheaper markets. Taranto’s median rent is €591/month with an 8.49% gross yield, while Bolzano’s rent is €1,598/month but yield drops to 3.88%. Milano sits in the middle on price but still looks expensive relative to rent, with a 4.50% gross yield.

For buyers, investors, and relocators, the message is clear: Italy isn’t one apartment market, but several distinct ones stacked on top of each other. Location changes everything — from entry price to income potential.
