The most surprising thing in Germany’s 2026 apartment market is how far München has pulled ahead of everyone else. With a median asking sale price of €642,394, it isn’t just number one — it’s in a league of its own, sitting well above the country’s next most expensive cities.

Frankfurt am Main and Freiburg im Breisgau form the next tier, but they still trail Munich by a wide margin. Frankfurt’s median asking price came in at €509,947, while Freiburg reached €498,961. That gap shows a market where premium pricing is concentrated in a handful of cities rather than spread evenly across the top end.

What’s also notable is how compressed yields are at these price levels. Munich’s gross yield was just 2.23%, and Frankfurt’s was 2.40%, which is pretty typical for trophy urban markets where capital values rise faster than rents. Munich also had 4,364 listings, giving this snapshot a solid sample size and reinforcing that the city’s price premium is broad-based, not just a one-off outlier.
